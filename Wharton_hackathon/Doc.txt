Winning a smart‑surveys hackathon with the provided travel‑review dataset

Evidence‑grounded micro‑surveys (1–2 follow‑ups), voice + text capture, and an explainable demo plan

Executive summary

I will win this hackathon by building the smallest credible “smart survey engine” that does three things better than a generic chatbot: it (a) detects exactly what is missing, stale, or contradictory about a specific property right now, (b) asks only one or two tightly controlled follow‑up questions inside the review flow, and (c) immediately shows a before/after property facts card so judges can see why the questions mattered.

Using the uploaded dataset, I can show real, dataset‑driven gaps. The property table includes structured amenity and policy fields (amenity lists by category; check‑in/out times; pet and children policies; check‑in instructions; “know before you go”), and the reviews table includes time‑stamped free‑text reviews and a JSON rating payload with sub‑ratings (e.g., check‑in, hotel condition, room cleanliness). fileciteturn1file0 From a quick exploratory pass over the review text in the last 180 days of data, “cleanliness” language is common, while mentions of factual but high‑value topics like Wi‑Fi reliability, shuttle availability, and several amenity/policy details are scarce—creating ideal opportunities for micro‑follow‑ups that improve coverage with minimal burden.

I will implement an evidence‑grounded pipeline: extract evidence from reviews (baseline regex/keyword + optional ABSA), calculate gap scores (missingness, staleness, contradiction/uncertainty), generate candidates (templates + constrained LLM paraphrase) and rank them using a utility‑vs‑friction objective. For grounding and explainability, I will adopt a lightweight RAG pattern so every asked question can cite “why” (e.g., “last mentioned 26 months ago”, “limited parking complaints”). RAG is widely used to mitigate outdated knowledge and hallucination by incorporating external sources, but research also shows groundedness is not guaranteed—even with retrieval—so I will keep strict schemas and verification checks. [1]

For voice input, I will provide speech capture + automatic transcription + transcript confirmation, because survey‑methodology evidence shows ASR can introduce transcription errors and even change meaning, and noise can materially degrade performance. [2] I will harden the system against LLM failure modes (bias and prompt injection), because bias/fairness surveys stress that LLMs can amplify social biases, and OWASP ranks prompt injection as a top GenAI risk. [3]

Dataset mapping to an attribute taxonomy and gap opportunities

What I will treat as the dataset’s “knowledge base” and “evidence stream”

I will map the dataset into two conceptual layers:

I will treat Description_PROC as the property knowledge base: it contains property identifiers, location fields, a high‑level “popular amenities” list, multiple amenity subcategory lists (e.g., internet, parking, food and drink), and key operational policies (check‑in/out times and narrative policy fields). fileciteturn1file0

I will treat Reviews_PROC as the evidence stream: it contains property identifiers, a review acquisition date (timestamp), review title and free text, and a rating field that encodes overall and sub‑ratings (with “0” representing missing sub‑ratings). fileciteturn1file0

This split is ideal for smart micro‑surveys because it naturally supports: (a) coverage questions (what has not been evidenced recently), (b) staleness questions (what might have changed), and (c) contradiction questions (what evidence disagrees).

Evidence‑grounded pipeline and MVP demo plan

System architecture I will implement

I will implement the pipeline as a tight loop optimised for demo clarity:

  C[Load recent reviews for property from Reviews_PROC] --> D[Evidence extraction<br/>baseline: regex/keywords<br/>upgrade: ABSA model/LLM extraction]

I will justify the “RAG + verification” design choice explicitly in my demo narrative: RAG is intended to improve accuracy/credibility by grounding generation in retrieved evidence, but groundedness studies show that even retrieval‑augmented long‑form generation can contain a significant fraction of ungrounded sentences—so I will constrain the model and use factual templates for the final question. [5]

Follow‑up question generation approach I will use

I will use “deterministic topic selection + controlled realisation”:

I will never let the model freely choose what to ask about; instead, the gap engine outputs a top attribute $a^\*$ (and optionally a second attribute if the first answer indicates uncertainty).

I will generate the final question text from a neutral template library, then optionally apply an LLM rewrite limited to tone/localisation (en‑GB wording), with a strict schema output. I will incorporate ideas from conversational survey research: Ge et al. define a knowledge‑driven follow‑up question generation task and propose reference‑free “Gricean‑inspired” evaluation metrics for clarity/coherence/informativeness, which I can adapt as offline checks (e.g., flag questions that are too vague or not answerable). [6]

Voice capture MVP I will demo and why transcript confirmation is non‑negotiable

I will implement a simple voice capture flow: “tap mic → record → transcribe → confirm → submit”.

This is not only good UX; it is methodological risk control. SurveyPractice research on ASR in web surveys shows that ASR transcription errors can partially change responses and that background noise can significantly impair ASR performance, so confirmation/correction is the fastest safeguard. [7] I will use Whisper as a practical baseline for robust speech recognition and cite the underlying Whisper paper as my technical foundation. [8]

The MVP demo I will deliver

I will demo three “judge‑visible” artefacts:

I will demo the review flow with a follow‑up card inserted at the end. The card will ask one targeted question with a “Skip” button and voice/text choice.

I will demo the explainability panel: “Why am I being asked?” will show staleness or contradiction evidence (e.g., “few mentions in last 24 months”; “parking availability complaints”). This defends against “generic chatbot” critiques and shows groundedness.

I will demo a property facts card preview that shows exactly which field is being updated (value, timestamp, confidence). This makes the benefit tangible in under 10 seconds.

How I will come up with the best survey questions

I will treat “best question selection” as a constrained optimisation task: I must spend a budget of 1–2 questions to maximise information gain and downstream usefulness while minimising friction and risk.

Principles I will enforce

I will enforce that every question is:

Answerable and specific (“Was free parking available on‑site during your stay?”) rather than vague (“How was parking?”).

Time‑bounded (“during your stay”) to reduce contradiction ambiguity.

Non‑leading and neutral; leading questions can sway responses, so I will avoid suggestive adjectives and asymmetric options. [15]

Grounded in evidence, not model imagination. RAG can help supply relevant evidence, but groundedness studies show retrieval does not guarantee faithfulness, so I will keep “topic selection” outside the LLM and validate outputs. [5]

Algorithms I will implement, from simplest MVP to stronger variants

Evidence extraction via ABSA and structured IE

I will start with a baseline extractor (keywords + regex + negation patterns) because it produces visible progress in hours.

I will then upgrade with ABSA / structured extraction if time allows. Hospitality‑domain ABSA resources such as HOSSemEval‑EB23 show that hospitality reviews can be annotated at aspect granularity, supporting the feasibility of aspect extraction for amenities and experience factors. [12]

Contradiction detection via NLI + clustering

I will represent candidate facts as short hypotheses (e.g., “Parking is available on‑site”, “Parking is limited”). I will classify review sentences against these hypotheses using an NLI model and aggregate into a distribution.

DeBERTaV3 is a common modern backbone for NLU tasks and has been used widely for inference settings; I will cite it as my NLI‑capable model family. [9]

Retrieval‑augmented generation for evidence‑backed phrasing

I will use RAG to retrieve property context (amenity strings and policy text) and the most recent, most relevant review snippets for the selected attribute, then generate a question and a one‑sentence rationale.

RAG surveys highlight motivations such as mitigating hallucinations and enabling continuous knowledge updates by integrating external sources. [4]

However, I will explicitly guard against the known limitation that RAG outputs can remain partially ungrounded; groundedness studies in retrieval‑augmented long‑form generation show a non‑trivial fraction of sentences can be ungrounded even when answers are correct, so I will keep question content template‑locked and use the model mainly for rewriting and rationale summarisation. [16]

Question selection as active learning and CAT under a micro‑budget

I will implement a ranker that approximates expected information gain with a small set of interpretable terms.

Active learning surveys provide a taxonomy of query strategies (uncertainty sampling, diversity sampling, cost‑aware selection) that maps cleanly to “which question should I ask next when I only get one shot?” [17]

To increase rigour, I will reference CAT literature: CAT’s core idea is dynamic item selection to maximise measurement efficiency, and ML‑focused CAT surveys describe the selection algorithm as the heart of adaptivity. [18] I will optionally cite recent RL‑based item selection work as inspiration if I discuss future directions (not required for MVP). [19]

Evaluation metrics and workflow I will present to judges

I will evaluate question quality both offline and in the live demo:

Offline, I will adapt conversational‑survey research metrics (clarity/coherence/informativeness) to automatically lint questions; Ge et al. provide a principled basis for reference‑free evaluation of follow‑up questions. [20]

For RAG, I will track retrieval relevance and generation faithfulness. RAG evaluation surveys (Auepora) describe evaluation dimensions across retrieval and generation, giving me vocabulary to justify my metrics. [21]

For voice, I will track transcript correction rate and WER proxies; survey evidence shows meaning can change with ASR, making confirmation essential. [7]

If I have time, I will borrow the mindset from ML‑assisted survey question assessment work: SurveyPractice’s CARI ML discussion frames ML pipelines as tools to flag poor‑performing questions during pilot/early fielding, motivating my “Question Optimisation Lab” component. [22]

What I will prioritise to “win” in judging

I will prioritise a story the judges can see: “We found a gap, we asked one question, and the property facts updated.” I will show this loop twice (two different gaps) rather than attempt to cover every attribute.

I will prioritise explainability because it distinguishes “smart surveys” from “LLM chat”: I will show why a question was selected (staleness, contradiction, missingness) and cite how conversational survey research evaluates follow‑up quality. [20]

I will prioritise safety and robustness because judges penalise demos that fail in edge cases: I will hard‑cap the system to 2 questions, keep templates, and implement prompt‑injection alignment following OWASP’s risk framing and recent prompt‑injection vulnerability evidence. [44]

I will prioritise voice quality safeguards because they are both user‑valuable and research‑backed: transcript confirmation is consistent with survey findings on ASR error patterns. [7]
